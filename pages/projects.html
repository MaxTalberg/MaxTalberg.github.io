Projects

My work spans astrophysics, machine learning, and computational physics. These diverse fields offer endless opportunities for discovery. Below, I describe some of the main projects I have been working on recently.

Gaussian Process Radial Velocity Modelling

Radial velocity signals are often buried in noise from stellar activity. During my MPhil thesis, I re-examined the putative detection of L98-59b by applying Gaussian Process models to disentangle stellar signals from planetary ones. This work yielded new insights into the system's dynamics and improved detection robustness.

These models have significant potential for application in other planetary systems and represent a powerful tool for refining exoplanet discoveries.

Kepler Exoplanet Analysis

The Kepler mission opened a window into the study of exoplanets. In this project, I focused on four potentially habitable planets, analyzing photometric data to assess their orbital parameters and atmospheric properties. This work involved advanced statistical techniques to extract meaningful insights from complex datasets.

Understanding these distant worlds not only feeds into larger cataloging efforts but also pushes us closer to identifying planets that might harbor life.

Custom Diffusion Models

Diffusion models have become a cornerstone of modern machine learning. I developed a Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) using the TensorFlow framework, training it on the MNIST dataset. This project showcased the flexibility of diffusion models in generating high-quality synthetic data and their potential application to more complex datasets.

The experience also provided me with hands-on expertise in training deep learning architectures, from data preparation to model optimization.

Runge-Kutta Simulations for Three-Body Dynamics

The Restricted Three-Body Problem presents an elegant challenge in celestial mechanics. I implemented Runge-Kutta numerical methods to simulate Lagrange points, where gravitational forces balance perfectly. These points have implications for spacecraft trajectory planning and understanding orbital stability in multi-body systems.

This simulation offered a fascinating glimpse into the complex interactions that govern planetary and satellite motion.

High-Performance Computing in Physics

Modern research increasingly relies on computational power. I have leveraged GPU-based high-performance computing resources for astrophysical simulations and deep learning projects. These include modeling photon scattering through planetary atmospheres and analyzing massive datasets from telescopes.

Such tools have not only accelerated my workflows but also enabled me to tackle computationally intensive problems with greater precision.
